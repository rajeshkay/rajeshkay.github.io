Summary

This page is intended to offer a brief overview about Stroke and a summary of the various known risk factors that can induce a stroke. More importantly, this page is intended to provide an opportunity to visualize and better understand the statistical distribution of the various factors of stroke among a small subset of victims. This page was created for the purpose of understanding/demonstrating narrative visualization. This webpage is neither accurate nor complete source of information about Stroke. .

What is Stroke?

The brain controls our movements, stores our memories, and is the source of our thoughts, emotions, and language. The brain also controls many functions of the body, like breathing and digestion.

To work properly, your brain needs oxygen. Your arteries deliver oxygen-rich blood to all parts of your brain. When something blocks blood supply to part of the brain or when a blood vessel bursts and that part of brain gets damaged, brain cells in that region start to die within minutes, because they canâ€™t get oxygen. This causes a stroke. A stroke can cause long-term disability, or even death.

The following webpages can provide a good start in better understanding of Stroke.

  • CDC - Stroke Home
  • Signs of Stroke
  • Reducing the Risk of Stroke
  • Wikipedia - About Stroke
Strokes can be classified into two major categories:
  • Ischemic: Ischemic strokes are caused by interruption of the blood supply to the brain.
  • Hemorrhagic: Hemorrhagic strokes result from the rupture of a blood vessel or an abnormal vascular structure.
About 87% of strokes are ischemic, the rest being hemorrhagic. Bleeding can develop inside areas of ischemia, a condition known as "hemorrhagic transformation." It is unknown how many hemorrhagic strokes actually start as ischemic strokes.

Strokes are a life-threatening emergency, and immediate medical attention is critical. When there is a doubt, one MUST consult a doctor immediately.

Risk Factors

According to the World Health Organization (WHO) stroke is the 2nd leading cause of death globally, responsible for approximately 11% of total deaths. Over the years, several factors have been suspected to induce Stroke. The picture on the right captures the most prominent risk factors of stroke.

Visualization of Risk Factors

Are there additional factors that can influence stroke? For example, the lifetime risk of stroke is higher for women than men, with a 1 in 4 risk of stroke for women after age 25. So, can gender be a factor or can age be a factor? In this section we will explore the influence of some of the well known factors as well as some new factors that are not prominently shown in the picture.

  • High blood pressure
  • Obesity
  • Heart Disease
  • Smoking
  • Age (factor not listed above)
  • Gender (factor not listed above)
  • Residence (factor not listed above)
In the visualizations that follow, one can explore the direct impact of these new factors and also visualize the influence of other risk factors when the dataset is categorized based on Age and Gender.

A small dataset of about 250 stroke victims has been chosen for the purpose of this study.

Obesity and Diabetes are already identified as risk factors for Stroke. BMI is a direct indication of Obesity and Higher blood glucose level indicates diabetes. One unit increase in body mass index (BMI) increases the risk of stroke by 5 percent. Diabetes can cause pathologic changes in blood vessels at various locations and can lead to stroke if cerebral vessels are directly affected. Additionally, mortality is higher and post stroke outcomes are poorer in patients with stroke with uncontrolled glucose levels. This section presents the visualization of BMI and blood glucose levels of adult stroke victims categorized by Age and Gender.

The y-axis of the chart represents Age of the victim at the time of stroke. The x-axis indicates the blood glucose levels. The size of the marks (circles) indicate the BMI of the stroke victim.

NOTE: This is an interactive chart. The reader can apply filters on different BMI levels or Gender or both. These filters help visualize the effect of blood glucose level and obesity for different gender and age.

Observation(s): In the given dataset, we can visualize the following (by applying filters -

  • Age is still a primary criteria for adults who are maintaining a Healthy Weight. However, heavier (Over Weight and/or Obese) adults are are more vulnerable to Stroke at an younger age. This dataset also conveys that Obesity as a key Risk Factor.
  • The impact of person's weight appears uniform across both Gender.
  • It appears that blood sugar level alone is not the key factor in this dataset. Obesity appears to influence much more than blood sugar level (in this dataset).


Someone who smokes 20 cigarettes a day is six times more likely to have a stroke compared to a non-smoker. Common heart disorders can increase your risk for stroke. For example, coronary artery disease increases your risk for stroke, because plaque builds up in the arteries and blocks the flow of oxygen-rich blood to the brain. Also, High blood pressure or Hypertension can cause the arteries that supply blood and oxygen to the brain to burst or be blocked, causing a stroke. This section offers an interactive visualization of smoking , blood pressure and heart disease for different age group across men and women. An additional (less common) risk factor Residence Type is also included in this chart.

This is a bidirectional chart. The x-axis of this chart shows different age groups. The y-axis of the chart provides the count of stroke victims. For each age group, there is a bar in the upward direction for Male and another for Female presenting the total number of stroke victims per Gender for that age group. Based on the selection of factor, the downward graph presents the count of men and women under different categories of the chosen factor. The color coding in this chart is uniformly applied across all risk factors (except residence type). Red implies that it is bad for health and green means it is either good for health or it does not have an impact to health. A different color scheme is chosen for factors that may not have a direct impact to health (like residence type).

The following sub-categories are present in each of the factor -

  • Smoking Status: When filtered on Smoking, 4 categories for smoking are depicted -
    • count of stroke victim who is not a smoker, represented using green
    • count of stroke victim who is an active smoker, represented using red
    • count of stroke victim who smoked in the past (not an active smoker), represented using orange
    • count of stroke victim whose smoking status is unknown represented using light blue
  • Heart Disease: When filtered on Heart Disease, the red portion of each downward bar indicates the count of men/women who have a pre-existing heart disease while the green portion provides the count of men/women without any heart issue.
  • Hypertension: When filtered on Hypertension, the red portion of each downward bar indicates the count of men/women who have high blood pressure or Hypertension while the green portion indicates a healthy blood pressure.
  • Residence Type: When filtered on Residence Type, aggregate measure is shown for 2 categories - sum of all stroke victims (men/women) residing in Urban area vs the sum of those living in rural area.
NOTE: This is a bidirectional interactive chart. The reader can apply filters on different factors to visualize the distribution of these factors for different gender and age.

Observation(s): In the given dataset, we can visualize the following (by applying filters) -
  • Filter on Smoking: Across all age groups, it appears that active smoking is not the greatest risk factor. However, combining active smokers and past smokers count, smoking appears as risk factor for Males across all age group and Females in some age group (Females between 50-70years of age).
  • Filter on Heart Disease: For the people in this dataset, there is no evidence that Heart Disease is a major risk factor for Stroke.
  • Filter on Blood Pressure: For the people in this dataset, there is no evidence that Blood Pressure is a major risk factor for Stroke.
  • Filter on Residence: Across all age groups and gender, people living in Urban areas have suffered from stroke than people living in Rural.
The dataset considered for this webpage is neither extensive or comprehensive. Hence, none of the above observations can be generalized as inferences.
